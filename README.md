# UrbanHeatIslands
Using US Climate Data to look at Heat Islands in the State of Pennsylvania
Executive Summary  

Urban heat islands occur in cities as heat is stored, absorbed or entrapped more than surrounding rural and suburban areas [1]. This intensifies with urbanization, increasing morbidity, and mortality [1]. For example, urban heat islands increase the risk of people dying of heat stroke and other heat related illnesses [1].  In America, these heat islands disproportionately impacts communities of color [1]. Thus, a comparison of Lebanon and Hershey Which contains different levels of community, colors and rates of urbanization, will show how these urban heat islands are forming within central PA.  

Motivation 

Urban heat islands have distinct spatial and temporal patterns, and oftentimes can intensify over time, creating intra urban heat island effects [1]. Urban heat islands have several effects on local climate, impacting local wind patterns, forming cloud and fog, increasing humidity, changing precipitation rate, and, of course, increasing the temperature of these areas [2]. Provisionally, heat islands are examined through hotspot analysis, ends, spacetime patterns, creating linear models, or analyzing these features through remote sensing. However, this has the limit of the resolution of data and data deficiencies [2]. Other methods of analyzing urban heat islands are field measurements and observation which have a weakness in statistical analysis and scale [2]. Using more predictive models and stimulation approaches is computationally difficult, requiring major simplifications and high cost [2].  

Data Question  

How do marginalized communities, specifically communities of color, experience weather anomalies in their geographical communities? Specifically, how much greater anomalies on heat islands when compared to county averages?
